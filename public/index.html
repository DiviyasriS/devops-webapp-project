What is a Qubit?

In classical computing, information is stored in bits that exist in one of two states: 0 or 1. A qubit, or quantum bit, is the fundamental unit of quantum information. Unlike classical bits, qubits can exist in a superposition of both states simultaneously, unlocking exponential computational power.

Superposition

Superposition is the quantum mechanical phenomenon where a qubit exists in multiple states at once. When a qubit is in superposition, it is simultaneously 0 and 1 until measured. This property allows quantum computers to process vast amounts of possibilities in parallel, providing exponential speedup for certain computational problems.

Qubit in Superposition

Spinning represents both 0 and 1 states existing simultaneously

The famous Hadamard gate (H) is the key to creating superposition. When applied to a qubit in state |0⟩, it transforms it into an equal superposition of |0⟩ and |1⟩, each with 50% probability upon measurement.

Quantum Gates

Quantum gates are the building blocks of quantum circuits. They manipulate qubits by performing unitary transformations, changing their quantum states. Unlike classical logic gates, quantum gates are reversible and preserve quantum information.

Hadamard Gate (H)

Creates superposition: transforms |0⟩ into (|0⟩ + |1⟩)/√2

Pauli-X Gate (X)

X

Quantum NOT gate: flips |0⟩ to |1⟩ and vice versa

Pauli-Z Gate (Z)

Z

Phase flip: applies -1 phase to |1⟩ state
